I take that as a compliment because at least I'm the best at something. Anyway. Water. Let's get into it before I ramble any more, which I will absolutely do.

At 110°F — which Ohio, Texas, and most of the Sun Belt regularly sees during a grid-down heat event — you can be in organ failure in under 8 hours. The three-day rule you learned in Boy Scouts assumes mild weather. Strip that assumption and the math gets ugly fast.

How do I know about dehydration? Because I almost died from it. Not in the Persian Gulf. Not pullin' half-drowned sailors out of pitch-black water at two in the morning for Uncle Sam while your daddy was home watchin' Hee Haw. No shame. None whatsoever. Big Hoss is an American hero. I owe him forty bucks. I ain't forgot. I just ain't paid yet. That's a different thing.

Now here's where I'm supposed to give you the informational part of the article, which the Data Dive Bar fellas keep on me about because apparently "Bobby goes on a fifteen-minute tangent about the mayor and never explains how to filter water" is not technically a survival guide. Fine. Here it is. Pond water, creek water, your neighbor's above-ground pool that's been a biology experiment since 2015 — you cannot drink it straight. You'll get parasites and gut bacteria and all manner of microscopic nightmare critters that will do to your insides exactly what Darryl's chicken fried steak did to mine, and brother, you do not want that. I been there. I know what that looks like. I know what it sounds like too, according to Big Hoss from Tulsa, who I apparently traumatized for life.

Three ways to fix it. Boil it — rolling boil, sixty seconds, done, everything biological that was tryin' to murder you is dead. Above sixty-five hundred feet, go three minutes on account of altitude physics, which I don't fully understand but the nerds do and that's good enough for me. Bleach — eight drops of plain unscented Clorox per gallon, wait thirty minutes, drink it, it'll taste like a YMCA locker room but you'll be alive and that's the goal. Or get a real filter, not a Brita, a Brita is a toy and if you show up to the apocalypse with a Brita I will personally laugh at you, I mean a Sawyer Squeeze or a Berkey, something that actually works. That's the whole water safety lecture. You're welcome. Now I need to talk about the truck for a second.

Gutters into food-grade barrels, filter what you collect. God put it up there for free and the government can mostly go to hell about it. Check your state laws. Ohio's fine. Some states are weird about it for dumb reasons.

Milk jugs. Buckets. Under the bed.
Bakery departments at grocery stores give away food-grade five-gallon buckets free if you ask. Fill 'em from the tap. Rotate every six months. Spread it around — shed, car, closet. Don't keep all your water in one place like an amateur.

Methodology & Sources

Dehydration survival timeline — Synthesized from peer-reviewed clinical literature including Nutrition Reviews, American Journal of Physiology, and NHS/Mayo Clinic clinical guidelines. The 72–96 hour baseline reflects adult survival under temperate conditions (60–75°F) without other stressors. Temperature survival windows based on sweat rate research (adults lose approximately 1–1.5 L/hr sweating at extreme heat) and clinical case literature.

Household preparedness statistics — 51% self-reported preparedness from FEMA 2023 National Household Survey (~7,600 respondents; community.fema.gov). 53% with 3-day water supply and 96% with 3-day food supply from Yadav & Kumar (2021), Disaster Medicine and Public Health Preparedness (PMID: 33820591), based on a national survey sample.

Water purification recommendations — Boil water and bleach treatment guidance consistent with CDC Emergency Water Disinfection guidelines. 8 drops unscented household bleach (5–9% sodium hypochlorite) per gallon, 30-minute contact time. Above 6,000 ft: boil 3 minutes. At or below 6,000 ft: boil 1 minute. Source: cdc.gov/water-emergency.
