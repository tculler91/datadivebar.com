You Will Die of Thirst Before You Die of Anything Else

A data-backed survival guide to not dying of thirst in your own driveway. Written by Bobby "Pinkeye" Duggan — 55, Navy rescue diver, Vietnam vet, twenty-year truck driver, and a man currently typing this on a laptop an orange cat pissed on — from a relationship he's still figuring out how to describe. He is qualified. He is also angry about it.

Data Dive Bar asked me to write these articles. I said no. They explained their reasoning. I explained that I haul auto parts in a Ford Econoline that smells like something died in the ventilation system around 2019 and nobody's gone looking, and that I type with two fingers on a laptop Brenda's orange cat Pumpkin pissed on somewhere around last March and neither of us has properly dealt with it. You open that lid and the smell hits you — cat ammonia, Brenda's open browser tabs, fifteen years of questionable decisions baked into the plastic. She named that cat Pumpkin. That cat is not a Pumpkin. That cat is a problem. I'm writing the articles anyway because the alternative is most of you dying embarrassingly.

The pipes under your city are old. Real old. The guys keeping them running are underpaid and one bad contract negotiation away from taking their expertise home and letting you figure it out. When something goes wrong — and something always goes wrong eventually — you've got maybe seventy-two hours before your faucet becomes decorative. FEMA knows this. They've got the numbers. The numbers are not good. Most Americans have nothing stored, no plan, and a vague sense that the government will show up with pallets of SmartWater before anything gets too bad. Bobby Duggan is going to be on the porch in his aviators with sixty gallons in the garage and no sympathy to spare. I'll feel a little bad. Not enough to do anything about it. That's your window — before the pipes fail — to not be the person crying outside a FEMA tent over a six-ounce water bottle at noon in July.

Fifty-five years old. Five nine. North of two hundred pounds, and how far north is between me, my doctor, and the Lord, and none of them are talking. U.S. Navy, rescue diver, Vietnam, '69 through '70. I pulled half-drowned men out of water so black and cold you couldn't tell which way the surface was — Tonkin Gulf, South China Sea, places that have the kind of dark that stays with you — and I did that for two years and then I came home and drove trucks for thirty more because I liked being alone with the radio and I was genuinely good at it.

I survived an F5 tornado on I-80 in Nebraska hauling forty thousand pounds of steel coils at eighty-five miles an hour. I mention this regularly. I'll mention it until they put me in the ground and then I want it on the stone. You run a funnel cloud the size of God's actual fist in a Peterbilt and see what you feel like talking about at parties. I also survived a divorce, which left scars in different places. I survived gas station sushi in Bakersfield off the 99 — clerk named Doug, four dollars, I should've known — and that experience was worse than both the tornado and Vietnam, and I've thought about it enough that I'm confident saying so out loud.

Currently surviving Brenda. Last Tuesday she looked at me over her coffee and said, with real calm, that I was "the most exhausting person she had ever spent significant time around in her life." I thought about it. Probably true. Also a separate problem from water, which is the thing we're here to talk about, so let's do that before I go somewhere else with this, which I will, that's just how I'm built.

At 110°F — which Ohio, Texas, and most of the Sun Belt regularly sees during a grid-down heat event — you can be in organ failure in under 8 hours. The three-day rule you learned in Boy Scouts assumes mild weather. Strip that assumption out and it all goes to hell in a handbasket faster than you can say "I shoulda listened to that crazy sumbitch with the truck."

I know about dehydration because I nearly died from it in a truck stop parking lot outside Kearney, Nebraska at about eleven in the morning on a Tuesday in August. Not Vietnam. Not rescue diving in the South China Sea with guys shooting at me from three directions. A parking lot in Nebraska. Six-dollar chicken fried steak. Cook named Darryl. I have thought about Darryl almost every day since 2011. I will die without forgiving him. That's a choice I've made and I'm at peace with it.

This was when I was still running big rigs. Real trucks, eighteen wheels, not this Econoline nonsense I do now hauling partial pallets to towns so far out they don't even have a Dairy Queen anymore, just sadness and corn. I stopped at an independent diner off I-80 — not a Pilot Flying J, and I need to be clear that Pilot Flying J has never once done me wrong and I have a bumper sticker that says so. This was some other place with a hand-lettered sign and a menu laminated so many times over the decades it was mostly just laminate with vague menu shapes underneath. I ordered the six-dollar chicken fried steak. Tasted like it'd been fried in whatever oil they used during the Ford administration and just topped off ever since. Fine. Sometimes that's lunch. You don't always get to choose.

About ninety minutes back on the road my digestive system filed a formal complaint. Not the normal kind. The kind where your body stops asking what you'd like to do and starts issuing directives. I made it to a rest area. That's where I stayed for forty-eight hours.

The sleeper cab bathroom is approximately the size of a coat closet that somebody welded onto a diesel engine as a punishment for something. Under good conditions it smells like recycled regret. These were not good conditions. Darryl's chicken fried steak was still processing. I had two sixteen-ounce bottles of Aquafina. One I drank. The other one I used for purposes that will stay between me, that rest area in Nebraska, and Almighty God, and Brenda is not going to find out because there is no reason she needs to know and I would like to keep it that way permanently.

By hour thirty-six I was in real trouble. Mouth like the inside of a shoe. Head splitting in a way that went past headache into something more structural, like my skull was reconsidering its options. Heart doing something fast and uneven that I wasn't requesting. I made deals with God — the rent situation with Brenda, stopping calling Gary a dipshit, finally doing that thing with the truck I keep saying I'll handle. God and I both understood those were negotiating positions and not commitments. He bailed me out anyway. I figure he counted the Navy years. Pulling half-drowned boys out of the South China Sea in the dark has to be worth a little credit at some point.

What I hallucinated before Big Hoss knocked on the door was Dale Earnhardt standing at my front bumper with his arms crossed, looking at me like I'd done something stupid, which I had. Dale told me to drink some water. I said I didn't have any. Dale shook his head and walked off, and right about then I understood I was in genuine danger, because hallucination Dale is usually more helpful than that. What actually happened was a trucker named Big Hoss from Tulsa knocked on my cab door because — his words — I was making sounds like "a dog that ate something real bad." Big Hoss handed me a gallon jug and two orange Gatorades that were warm as soup. I sat outside my rig in that parking lot in the sun and cried for about twenty minutes like a man who has just been rescued from something, which I had been. I owe Big Hoss forty dollars. He knows. I know. Some debts you carry a while.

You will die of thirst before you die of hunger — that ain't philosophy, that's biology — and yet every one of you dipshits has seventeen cans of Campbell's in that cabinet and about twelve ounces of tap water you ran through a Brita like it's gonna save anybody. You got soup for a month and water for about a Tuesday. How's that plan workin' out for ya? That's what I thought.

"One gallon per person per day. That is the whole goddamn lecture. Four people, two weeks, fifty-six gallons minimum. Carve it into your forehead if you have to. Tell it to your dog. I do not give a single solitary damn how you remember it, just remember it."

Now I bet half of you turkeys are sittin' there right now thinkin' everything is fine and dandy. Rainbows and unicorns comin' right out of your ass. "The city water works great, Bobby. The pipes are totally fine." Yeah, that's what people always think — right up until a water main bursts, a chemical spill gets into the reservoir, or a grid failure knocks out the pumps. Then it ain't rainbows. It's you and your family starin' at a dry faucet wondering why you didn't listen to the crazy sumbitch with the inoperable truck.

The Data Dive Bar fellas keep nudging me to get to the actual information. Apparently "Bobby rants about the mayor for twelve minutes and never explains filtration" is not a survival guide. FINE. Here it is. Pond water, creek water, your neighbor's above-ground pool that's been growin' something since the Obama administration — you cannot drink it straight. You will get parasites and gut bacteria and all kinds of microscopic nightmare critters that'll do to your insides exactly what Darryl's chicken fried steak did to mine. I been there. I know what that looks like and what it sounds like, according to Big Hoss from Tulsa who was traumatized for life just knockin' on a cab door.

Boil it. Rolling boil, sixty seconds, and everything biological that had plans to kill you is dead. Above six thousand feet, go three minutes, because altitude does something weird to the boiling point that the nerds understand and I don't. I didn't go to college. I went to Vietnam. But the Navy taught me to trust the nerds, so I do. God bless every last nerd on this green earth.

Bleach. Plain unscented Clorox. Eight drops per gallon if the water's clear. Sixteen if it looks like you scooped it out from under something dead — and yes, that has happened to me in my life, and no, I don't want to talk about it right now. Stir it, wait thirty minutes, drink it. It'll taste like a YMCA locker room but you'll be alive and that is the whole point. Quit your bellyachin'. I once drank radiator runoff in a Walmart parking lot in Tulsa, Oklahoma, because that was my only available option at that particular moment in my life. Compared to radiator runoff, bleach water tastes like a goddamn piña colada on a white sand beach. Don't drink radiator runoff though. That's free advice. I'm full of free advice and none of it came from a comfortable place.

Filters. Get a real one. Not a Brita. A Brita is a toy. A Brita is what people in nice suburbs buy so they can feel like they're roughin' it while they drink tap water in a two-hundred-thousand-dollar kitchen and congratulate themselves. That is a load of malarkey and I will die on that hill. I'm talkin' about a Sawyer Squeeze. A LifeStraw. A Berkey counter unit that looks like R2-D2's uglier brother. Something that actually filters bacteria and parasites and all manner of microscopic bullshit. The only thing a good filter can't remove is the look on Brenda's daddy Jack's face when I come downstairs every morning and eat his eggs. That would require either therapy or a complete absence of shame. I went with option two. It's working great. No regrets whatsoever.

"Mr. Duggan, please remove the inoperable vehicle." Mr. Duggan this, Mr. Duggan that. Gary, when I was sixteen years old I built my high school's football stadium with nothing but a John Deere tractor and a disc plow because nobody else had the balls to do it. Coach Dempsey, God rest his soul, stood there and watched me grade that entire field and said, "Bobby, you're the only kid I got who ain't completely useless." That is called CHARACTER, Gary. That truck ain't moving. You know what's in it right now? Thirty-two bottles of water in the cab. Twelve more in the bed, underneath the volunteer trees that've been growing out of the cargo floor for several years now, which I consider to be nature personally endorsing my situation. The possums living under the front axle can stay too. They're doing fine. My point is: when this town's water main finally gives out — and it WILL, that thing is older than I am and twice as corroded and held together with nothin' but luck and local government incompetence — every one of them thirty-seven petition-signing turkeys is gonna come running up Brenda's driveway, hats in hand, acting like they didn't just spend two years trying to have my supply depot towed to a junkyard. I might share my water. MIGHT. That depends entirely on whether you signed that list and whether you're willing to apologize directly to my face and tell Gary to go pound sand. I memorized every name on that petition. Don't think I didn't, Gary.

Then there's Jack. Brenda's daddy. That man looks at me the way a cat looks at a bathtub — pure dread, no wiggle room, complete absence of goodwill. He knows I don't pay rent. He knows about the truck. He knows I eat his food and sleep under his roof and contribute, in his exact words, "absolutely goddamn nothing" to the household. There's tools in that shed he's been waitin' on me to use to fix things around the house since approximately 2019. Jack, I get it. I understand your frustration. But I pulled boys outta the South China Sea for this country while you were selling auto parts in Coshocton County, and I think that earns a man a few eggs. I ain't leaving. The water supply ain't leaving. We're all gonna be just fine.

Some of you from around town are reading this thinking, "Bobby, ain't you the guy who won fifty grand on a scratch-off?" Let me be crystal clear. I do not know what anybody is talking about. I buy scratch-offs at the gas station like a normal American. Some hit, some don't. Whatever the IRS may or may not be looking into is nobody's business. And Brenda is LYING about the proposal. I never got down on one knee. She turned down nothing because there was nothing to turn down. My face is red right now because I am angry, not embarrassed — those are completely different things — and the subject is closed and I will not elaborate further. We are done with this topic.

Here's what I need you to take away. Water kills you faster than anything else. Three days is your entire window and that's the whole game. One gallon per person per day, not a drop less — four people, two weeks, that's fifty-six gallons minimum and don't let any dipshit talk you down from that number. Your water heater is sitting in your basement right now with forty to eighty gallons you completely forgot about. Turn the power off first or you will die stupid, and dying stupid is worse than dying thirsty, in my professional opinion as a man who has been close to both. Boil it, bleach it, or filter it. Catch rainwater off your roof because God made it free and the government can go to hell about it — except in Ohio where it's legal, thank the sweet baby Jesus, because if it wasn't I woulda snapped years ago.

Don't eat anything from a cook named Darryl. Methodology & Sources

Dehydration survival timeline — Synthesized from peer-reviewed clinical literature including Nutrition Reviews, American Journal of Physiology, and NHS/Mayo Clinic clinical guidelines. The 72–96 hour baseline reflects adult survival under temperate conditions (60–75°F) without other stressors. Temperature survival windows based on sweat rate research (adults lose approximately 1–1.5 L/hr sweating at extreme heat) and clinical case literature.

Household preparedness statistics — 51% self-reported preparedness from FEMA 2023 National Household Survey (~7,600 respondents; community.fema.gov). 53% with 3-day water supply and 96% with 3-day food supply from Yadav & Kumar (2021), Disaster Medicine and Public Health Preparedness (PMID: 33820591), based on a national survey sample.

Water purification recommendations — Boil water and bleach treatment guidance consistent with CDC Emergency Water Disinfection guidelines. 8 drops unscented household bleach (5–9% sodium hypochlorite) per gallon, 30-minute contact time. Above 6,000 ft: boil 3 minutes. At or below 6,000 ft: boil 1 minute. Source: cdc.gov/water-emergency.
