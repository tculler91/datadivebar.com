Because if everything goes sideways tomorrow — and it WILL, this country's water infrastructure is held together with duct tape and the stubborn pride of underpaid pipe workers who are one bad contract negotiation away from walkin' off the job — most of you turkeys are gonna be standin' around like headless chickens at a FEMA tent, wonderin' where the free water's at. Bobby "Pinkeye" Duggan is gonna be sittin' on Brenda's porch with enough stored water to last till Easter, happier than pigs in shit, wearin' his aviator sunglasses. I also survived an F5 tornado on I-80 in Nebraska haulin' forty thousand pounds of steel coils doin' about eighty-five. I bring that up constantly and I will continue to bring it up until I die. You outrun a funnel cloud the size of God's clenched fist in a Peterbilt and then we can have a conversation about what a man's allowed to mention frequently. Until then, sit down and listen.

I survived a divorce. And I am currently survivin' a relationship with Brenda, who told me last Tuesday I'm "the most exhaustin' human being on planet Earth." I take that as a compliment because at least I'm number one at somethin'.

Anyway. Water. Let's get into it before I go sideways, which I absolutely will, and I apologize for nothin'.

At 110°F — which Ohio, Texas, and most of the Sun Belt regularly sees during a grid-down heat event — you can be in organ failure in under 8 hours. The three-day rule you learned in Boy Scouts assumes mild weather. Strip that assumption out and it all goes to hell in a handbasket faster than you can say "I shoulda listened to that crazy sumbitch with the truck."

I know about dehydration because I almost died from it. Not in Vietnam. Not pullin' half-drowned sailors outta black water at two in the goddamn morning for Uncle Sam while your daddy was home watchin' Hee Haw. I almost died in a truck stop parking lot outside Kearney, Nebraska, because of a six-dollar chicken fried steak and a cook named Darryl. I have not forgiven Darryl. I will never forgive Darryl. That man's cooking changed my insides in ways a therapist couldn't fix.

This was 2011. Still runnin' big rigs back then. Real trucks. Eighteen wheels. Not this Econoline horseshit I do now, haulin' half-pallets of God-knows-what to towns so far out in the sticks they might as well be in Bumfuck, Ohio, which is the same as Bumfuck, Egypt, but with more soybeans and less history. August. Hotter than two rats screwin' in a wool sock. Hotter than Satan's armpit on a double shift. I stop at this sad little diner off the highway — not a Pilot Flying J, which was mistake number goddamn one, Pilot Flying J has never once done me wrong and I got a bumper sticker about it — and I order the chicken fried steak. Six bucks. Tasted like it'd been fried in whatever grease was left in the pan from the Carter administration. Fine. You shut your mouth and eat it.

Three hours later my guts started making noises like a garbage disposal full of gravel and broken regrets. That's when I knew.

What Darryl's cooking did to my insides is something I will take to my grave. I'll just say I was trapped in the sleeper cab bathroom for forty-eight straight hours. TWO. FULL. DAYS. The sleeper cab bathroom is approximately the size of a broom closet that somebody welded to a diesel engine — it smells like regret even on a good day — and these were not good days. My body declared total war on itself. Every organ I owned was tryin' to get out. It was like a fire drill except the fire was inside me and there was no exit and Darryl was the one who started it. I had two bottles of Aquafina. Two. I used one to try to stay alive. I used the other one for sanitation purposes the Geneva Convention probably has opinions on. Brenda still doesn't know. Let's keep it that way.

By hour thirty-six I was in deep shit. Literally and in every other sense of the word. My mouth was dryer than a popcorn fart on a hot August day. Head poundin' like a son of a bitch. Heart doin' some weird thing I did not appreciate one bit. That's when you start makin' deals with the Lord. I said, "God Almighty, you get me outta this bathroom alive, I swear I'll pay Brenda rent. I'll stop callin' Gary a dipshit. I'll do something about the truck." God and I both knew I was lyin' through every last tooth in my head. He bailed me out anyway — probably on account of all those Navy boys I fished outta the South China Sea. That kind of thing's gotta be worth some credit upstairs.

What actually saved my sorry ass was a trucker named Big Hoss from Tulsa, Oklahoma. He knocked on my cab door because — quoting him directly — he could hear me "making sounds like a cow getting run through a wood chipper." Big Hoss gave me a gallon jug and two warm orange Gatorades from his passenger seat. I ugly-cried for twenty solid minutes outside my rig in a Nebraska truck stop at noon like a complete goddamn disaster of a human person. No shame. None. Big Hoss is an American hero. The last thing I hallucinated before Big Hoss showed up was Dale Earnhardt. Leaning against my front bumper, arms crossed, shaking his head at me, telling me to drink some goddamn water. Rest in peace, number 3. I'm gettin' emotional. We're movin' on.

The Data Dive Bar fellas keep nudging me to get to the actual information. Apparently "Bobby rants about the mayor for twelve minutes and never explains filtration" is not a survival guide. FINE. Here it is. Pond water, creek water, your neighbor's above-ground pool that's been growin' something since the Obama administration — you cannot drink it straight. You will get parasites and gut bacteria and all kinds of microscopic nightmare critters that'll do to your insides exactly what Darryl's chicken fried steak did to mine. I been there. I know what that looks like and what it sounds like, according to Big Hoss from Tulsa who was traumatized for life just knockin' on a cab door.

Boil it. Rolling boil, sixty seconds, and everything biological that had plans to kill you is dead. Above six thousand feet, go three minutes, because altitude does something weird to the boiling point that the nerds understand and I don't. I didn't go to college. I went to Vietnam. But the Navy taught me to trust the nerds, so I do. God bless every last nerd on this green earth.

Bleach. Plain unscented Clorox. Eight drops per gallon if the water's clear. Sixteen if it looks like you scooped it out from under something dead — and yes, that has happened to me in my life, and no, I don't want to talk about it right now. Stir it, wait thirty minutes, drink it. It'll taste like a YMCA locker room but you'll be alive and that is the whole point. Quit your bellyachin'. I once drank radiator runoff in a Walmart parking lot in Tulsa, Oklahoma, because that was my only available option at that particular moment in my life. Compared to radiator runoff, bleach water tastes like a goddamn piña colada on a white sand beach. Don't drink radiator runoff though. That's free advice. I'm full of free advice and none of it came from a comfortable place.

Filters. Get a real one. Not a Brita. A Brita is a toy. A Brita is what people in nice suburbs buy so they can feel like they're roughin' it while they drink tap water in a two-hundred-thousand-dollar kitchen and congratulate themselves. That is a load of malarkey and I will die on that hill. I'm talkin' about a Sawyer Squeeze. A LifeStraw. A Berkey counter unit that looks like R2-D2's uglier brother. Something that actually filters bacteria and parasites and all manner of microscopic bullshit. The only thing a good filter can't remove is the look on Brenda's daddy Jack's face when I come downstairs every morning and eat his eggs. That would require either therapy or a complete absence of shame. I went with option two. It's working great. No regrets whatsoever.

Methodology & Sources

Dehydration survival timeline — Synthesized from peer-reviewed clinical literature including Nutrition Reviews, American Journal of Physiology, and NHS/Mayo Clinic clinical guidelines. The 72–96 hour baseline reflects adult survival under temperate conditions (60–75°F) without other stressors. Temperature survival windows based on sweat rate research (adults lose approximately 1–1.5 L/hr sweating at extreme heat) and clinical case literature.

Household preparedness statistics — 51% self-reported preparedness from FEMA 2023 National Household Survey (~7,600 respondents; community.fema.gov). 53% with 3-day water supply and 96% with 3-day food supply from Yadav & Kumar (2021), Disaster Medicine and Public Health Preparedness (PMID: 33820591), based on a national survey sample.

Water purification recommendations — Boil water and bleach treatment guidance consistent with CDC Emergency Water Disinfection guidelines. 8 drops unscented household bleach (5–9% sodium hypochlorite) per gallon, 30-minute contact time. Above 6,000 ft: boil 3 minutes. At or below 6,000 ft: boil 1 minute. Source: cdc.gov/water-emergency.
